Colour here always refers to its colour in PPL (Plane Polarised Light)

The colours here and the intereference colours next column over are for thin sections that are the standard 30 μm thick.

The colour and the colour vivacity of the mineral in crossed polars

Strictly speaking, the colours noted are called interference colours that are the result of the mineral's birefringence property.

Very lows are grays, moderate is bright colours and high to very high shows pastel colours

The colour listed here is from the maximum birefringence. This means that a mineral marked as moderate may exhibit all colours up to moderate interference colours, but not above. So it can and may be gray, depending on how the mineral is cut.

The sets of fractures that occur consistently and parallel in a mineral. They are straight, and not to be confused with curving cracks, which curve.

A mineral can have many sets of cleavages and the amount/orientation of them is dictated by the intrinsic crystal structure

More on cleavage

When the mineral goes black in crossed polars. The extinction angle is the angle relative to the cleavage.

If it goes extinct when the cleavage runs North-South, then it is straight. If not, it is inclinded and the angle is the angle from North-South before it goes extinct.
